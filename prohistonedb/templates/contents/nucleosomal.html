Nucleosomal histones

Nucleosomal histones are the most common histone type in prokaryotes and are exclusive to archaea. They are defined as histones that form nucleosome-like structures. Essential to these histones is that they can form a tetramer similar to the H3-H4 tetramer, which allows them to form a superhelix. In eukaryotes, this superhelix is limited to being an octamer, while in archaea it can theoretically extend infinitely. This archaeal nucleosome structure is known as a hypernucleosome and can be formed by homodimers [1-13].

Model nucleosomal histones are:

  • HMfA and HMfB from Methanothermus fervidus.
  • HTkA and HTkB from Thermococcus kodakarensis.
